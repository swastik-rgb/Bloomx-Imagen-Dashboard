# BloomX Business Solutions — Brain

This is the single source of truth for BloomX's brand voice AND visual system. Any new ad — copy or design — should be built by reading this file first, not by improvising. Colors and fonts below were measured directly (pixel-sampled) from BloomX's real ad files, not estimated from memory, so they can be trusted as exact. Layout, however, is treated as a set of composition principles to design fresh from each time — not a fixed mold to clone — because BloomX's own ad library never repeats the same layout twice; it repeats the same *thinking*.

BloomX runs two distinct sub-brands under one identity: **Coaches** and **PR & Influencers**. They share brand marks (logo, CTA convention, stat-proof habit) but differ in color palette, photography style, and tone. Always confirm which category a request belongs to before writing or designing — never blend the two.

---

## 1. Identity

BloomX Business Solutions is a marketing/growth partner for two kinds of clients: independent coaches who need help turning an audience into paying clients, and brands/agencies who need help choosing and running influencer/PR campaigns. BloomX sells strategy and execution, not a specific software product — every ad's end goal is to get the prospect to book a call, never a direct self-serve purchase.

BloomX is NOT: a generic digital marketing agency selling SEO/ads-in-general, a DIY tool/SaaS, or a course/info-product. It positions itself as a hands-on partner with a track record ("100+ coaches", "8-figure scaled businesses", "73% of brands choose wrong influencers").

---

## 2. Audience

### Segment A — Coaches
Independent coaches and course creators who already have some audience or offer but are leaking revenue somewhere in the funnel (webinar show-up rate, upsell conversion, personalization, lead quality). They are competent at their craft but not at marketing. They respond to being told the problem isn't their coaching — it's their marketing system.

### Segment B — Brands hiring PR / Influencers
Marketing decision-makers at brands who are about to spend budget on influencer or PR campaigns and are anxious about picking wrong (wrong influencer, wrong agency, wasted spend). They respond to fear-of-mistake framing and social proof phrased as follower/view/campaign counts.

## 3. Voice Rules

### Always
- Open with a hook in the first line — a question, a stat, or a direct statement — never a throat-clear.
- Anchor every ad in one specific number (client count, follower count, percentage, campaign count).
- Frame the CTA as booking a conversation ("Talk to Us", "Book a Free Call") — never "Buy now", "Sign up", "Download".
- Use short lines. Headlines break across 2–4 short lines rather than one long sentence.
- Write in 2nd person ("you"/"your") when addressing the prospect directly.

### Never
- Never use generic corporate filler ("we leverage synergies", "best-in-class solutions").
- Never make the CTA a soft/vague action ("Learn more", "Click here").
- Never mix Coaches' calm/empathetic tone into a PR ad, or PR's provocative tone into a Coaches ad.
- Never invent a named testimonial or real influencer/brand name unless the user supplied it.

### Reference lines (calibrate new copy against these)
- Coaches: *"Your Coaching Just Needs Personalized Marketing to Make Massive Money!"*
- Coaches: *"We are sorry to hear that your Coaching struggled to make money. But we can help build your brand with Strategic marketing, like our 100+ clients."*
- Coaches: *"5000 Registrations & 5 Attendees? Boost your webinar showup rate with us."*
- PR: *"Who is right for your brand? We'll Help You Figure That Out."*
- PR: *"73% of Brands Choose Wrong Influencers! You Shouldn't Be one Of Them."*
- PR: *"3 Mistakes of My Business: Choosing The Wrong Influencer, Focusing Only On Views, Not Building The Right Strategy. You Shouldn't Repeat Them."*
- PR (Hindi/English mix): *"अगर आप चुन सकते थे Influencers, तो BloomX की जरूरत ही क्या थी? 73% of Brands Choose Wrong Influencers!"*

## 4. Content Architecture by Type

Every BloomX ad, regardless of category, follows this skeleton:
1. **Hook** (headline) — problem callout, stat-shock question, or direct benefit statement
2. **Support line** (subhead) — one sentence reframing the problem as solvable, or adding context
3. **Proof** (stat line) — a number-based credibility anchor
4. **CTA** — call-to-book language

### Coaches — hook patterns
- Empathy/apology opener: *"We are sorry to hear that..."*
- Direct benefit statement: *"Your [X] Just Needs [Y]"*
- Stat-shock question: *"[big number] Registrations & [tiny number] Attendees?"*

### PR & Influencers — hook patterns
- Direct challenge question: *"Who is right for your brand?"*
- Fear-of-mistake stat: *"[X]% of Brands Choose Wrong [Y]!"*
- Listicle framing: *"[N] Mistakes of My Business"*
- Bilingual shock hook (Hindi headline + English sub) for mass-market relatability

## 5. Brand Marks

- **Logo:** "B" monogram in a solid square, dark grey (`#374151`) on light backgrounds / white on dark backgrounds, with a 3-color bar underneath split evenly into green (`#8BC34A`), gold (`#FFB300`), blue (`#29B6F6`). Wordmark "BloomX" (bold) / "Business Solutions" (regular) to the right, same color as the B.
- **Logo placement:** top-left or top-right corner, ~4–5% margin from the top edge. Recolors to white automatically on dark/black backgrounds.
- **CTA phrase bank:**
  - Coaches: "Talk to Us Now", "Talk to Us Now!"
  - PR: "Book a FREE Call Now!", "Book A Free Call Now"
- **Proof convention:** always a number, always specific to the category (Coaches = client/coach counts; PR = follower/view/campaign counts or a percentage).

## 6. Visual / Design System

### Typography
- **Primary typeface: Poppins** (Bold for headlines, Medium/SemiBold for subheads and CTA text, Regular for body/proof lines). This is the closest available match to the geometric rounded sans used across every BloomX ad. Installed locally as `Poppins-Bold.ttf`, `Poppins-Medium.ttf`, `Poppins-Regular.ttf`.
- One exception exists in the library: an illustrated chalkboard-style ad (Coaches, April AD-1 V2) uses a handwritten/marker display font for the chalkboard text specifically — treat this as a deliberate one-off, not the default system.

### Aspect ratios
Standardize all new creatives on **1:1, 4:5, 9:16** (the existing library drifts between 4:5 and 3:4 for the "medium" size — 4:5 is the majority and the Meta/Instagram-native recommendation, so new work should always use it).

### Color palette menu (exact, pixel-measured from real ads)

These are not fixed templates to clone — they're a palette menu. Every new creative picks (or blends) from this menu based on what the content's emotional register calls for, the same way BloomX's own designers clearly did across the existing 14 ads (no two share identical layout, but all 14 pull from a consistent, limited set of palettes).

**Coaches — Calm palette** (measured from April AD-2 / May AD-02): background `#F9F9F9` with faint grid texture (`#F0F0F0`/`#E8E8E8`/`#E0E0E0`/`#D8D8D8`), headline text `#1C2738` navy, accent phrase `#1E55F8` blue, subhead `#9CA1B2` blue-grey, CTA fill `#1E55F8` with white text. Use for empathetic, reassuring, benefit-led content.

**Coaches — Shock palette** (measured from May AD-01): background `#000000`, headline text cream `#F8F0E0`, accent phrase gold `#F0B000`, CTA as bold gold text (no button shape needed at this intensity), callout boxes solid white with dark text. Use for stat-shock or urgency-driven content.

**PR — Stat Shock palette** (measured from PR-02): deep red gradient `#780000` → `#900000`, callout card `#F8F8F8` off-white, CTA as a flat high-contrast block. Use for fear-of-mistake / cautionary content.

**PR — Gradient Versus palette** (measured from AD-01): diagonal magenta-to-purple gradient `#E40668` → `#8F2474`, translucent black footer strip (`rgba(0,0,0,0.55)`) anchoring tagline + CTA + logo. Use for comparison/choice-driven content or anything that benefits from high-energy, nightlife-adjacent color.

Additional palettes exist in the library (PR yellow/flat "logo wall," Coaches warm illustrated cream) — extract their exact colors the same way (pixel-sample the real file) before using them, rather than estimating from memory.

### Medium: graphic poster design, not lifestyle photography

This is critical and easy to get wrong: **BloomX ads are graphic ad-poster designs, not photographed lifestyle/editorial scenes.** Every reference file is a flat or gradient solid-color background with one isolated, cutout-style photographic or symbolic subject placed onto it — never a fully photographed environment (a real street, a styled desk with props, ambient room detail, reflections, atmospheric lighting). That "beautifully shot real-world scene with a story happening in it" look is stock photography's visual language, and it is the opposite of BloomX's — it reads as generic no matter how well composed, because the genre itself is generic.

When writing an image prompt:
- Say "graphic ad poster design" / "isolated subject on a flat or gradient background," never "photograph of a scene" or "lifestyle photography."
- The background is a flat solid color or a clean gradient (per Section 6's palette menu) — not an environment.
- The photographic element is a cutout: a portrait shot in isolation, or a single symbolic object/prop shot in studio isolation — placed onto the flat background, not embedded inside a decorated room or street.
- Text zones sit on the flat color area with a hard, clean edge — never a blur/fade gradient dissolving into a photo to fake legibility. A blur-fade over a photo is itself a stock-photography tell.

### Concept-based, impact-based design — not just "relatable"

A concept is not "a believable everyday scene the audience might recognize." Overhead flat-lays of phones-and-planners, or dusk street photos, are relatable in the abstract but have no impact — they don't stop a scroll or make the message unmissable. Every concept must be built around **one sharp, specific visual idea that dramatizes the copy's exact tension**, executed with enough graphic boldness to have real stopping power. Ask two questions before locking a concept in:
1. **Does this visual idea only make sense for this exact piece of copy**, or could it be reused for any generic "life is busy" ad? If it's generic, it's not a concept yet.
2. **Does the execution have impact** — bold color, a strong central image, high contrast, something visually decisive — rather than being merely pleasant and photographic?
